Exchange Rates

Definition

  • The price of one country's currency in terms of another country's currency

Foreign Exchange Market

  • Market where the currency of other countries is bought and sold

Measurements

Bilateral

Definition

  • The bilateral exchange rate measures the value of one country's currency in terms of another country's currency
  • Also called the cross exchange rate

Example

  • For example, AUD in terms of USD
    • 1 AUD = 0.90 USD
    • 1 USD = 0.71 British pounds

Quoting

  • Most commonly quoted exchange rate is the bilateral exchange rate against USD
  • There are two ways to quote a currency using the bilateral exchange rate:
    • In terms of a country's purchasing power
      • 1 AUD = 0.75 USD
    • In terms of a common currency
      • 1 USD = 1.33 AUD

Trade Weighted Index

Definition

  • The trade weighted index is a measure of movements in the Australian dollar against a weighted average of 20 other currencies
  • The currencies are weighted according to their importance to Australia's international trade flows

Advantages Over Bilateral Measure

  • TWI is more accurate and more significant than the bilateral exchange rate holistically
    • Though the bilateral rate is more significant for an individual travelling to South Africa, for example
  • As it is trade weighted, it relates to changes in performance of Australia's balance of payments over time

Formula

  • Price of good in foreign currency
    • Multiply price of good in domestic currency by the bilateral rate, quoted in terms of the domestic currency (ie. domestic currency is 1)
  • Price of good in domestic currency
    • Multiply price of good in foreign currency by the bilateral rate, quoted in terms of the foreign currency (ie. foreign currency is 1)

Example

  • Price in foreign currency
    • Given a good costs $60 AUD
    • The exchange rate is 1 AUD = 0.75 USD
    • The good costs \(60 \times 0.75 = 45\) USD
  • Price in domestic currency
    • Given a good costs $80 USD
    • The exchange rate is 1 AUD = 0.75 USD
    • The exchange rate in terms of the USD is 1 USD = \(\frac{1}{0.75}\) = 1.33 AUD
    • The good costs \(80 \times 1.33 = 106.4\) AUD

Model

  • The exchange rate is determined by supply and demand of the currency in the foreign exchange market
  • Whenever a firm or individual needs to move money into another country, it usually needs to convert the money into the country's currency
    • This involves a transaction in the foreign exchange market
  • All credits in the country's balance of payments represent demand for the currency
    • A credit requires converting foreign currency into Australian dollars
    • This is a purchase of Australian dollars (ie. demand for it)
  • All debits represent supply of the currency
    • A debit requires converting Australian dollars into foreign currency to perform a transaction overseas
    • This is the sale of Australian dollars (ie. supplying it to the market)

Increased Demand for the AUD

  • Increased demand for Australian exports
    • Exports are sold in AUD
    • Overseas consumers require AUD to purchase the exports
    • These consumers must therefore purchase AUD (demand for the dollar)
    • Examples: purchase of iron ore, overseas students studying in Australia, overseas tourists travelling to Australia
  • Increased foreign investment into Australia
    • Foreign investment in Australia requires AUD to perform domestic transactions
    • Overseas investors must convert their money into AUD to perform these transactions
    • Requires the purchase of AUD
    • Examples: construction of new mining infrastructure by an overseas mining company in Australia
  • Increased income receipts for Australians working overseas
    • Australians are paid in AUD
    • Companies hiring Australians must convert the income for these employees into AUD
    • Requires the purchase of AUD
  • Increased dividend payments to Australians who have invested in overases companies
    • Dividend payments must be converted to Australian dollars before being paid to Australian investors
    • Requires purchasing AUD
  • Increased profit flows to Australian companies with operations overseas
    • Profit flows are converted to Australian dollars before being returned to multinational companies with their headquaters in Australia

Increased Supply of the AUD

  • Increased spending on imports
    • Imports in other countries are sold in that country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency
    • This is the sale of AUD in the foreign exchange market, suppling AUD to the market
    • Examples: Purchase of cars from Japan, Australian tourists travelling overseas, Australian students studying in overseas universities
  • Increased foreign investment in other countries
    • Transactions in foreign countries that arise from investment in those countries take place in the foreign country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency
    • Examples: an Australian company building a manufacturing plant in China
  • Increased income payments to overseas residents working in Australia
    • Overseas residents are paid in their country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD to foreign currency
  • Increased dividend payments to overseas investors who have invested in Australian companies
    • Overseas investors expect to be paid in their country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency
  • Increased profit flows to overseas companies with operations in Australia
    • Overseas companies will convert profit made from operations in Australia into the currency of the country the company's headquarters are in
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency

Movements

Appreciation

  • Rise in the bilateral exchange rate for a country relative to another (usually the USD)
  • Or a rise in a country's trade weighted index

Depreciation

  • Fall in the bilateral exchange rate for a country relative to another (usually the USD)
  • Or a fall in a country's trade weighted index

Fixed

Definition

  • Where the price of a country's currency is set at a particular value and maintained by the country's central bank
  • Central bank may change the value they set the currency at
    • Increasing the value of the currency is called a revaluation
    • Decreasing the value of the currency is called a devaluation

Disadvantages

  • Requires repeated intervention by the central bank to adjust the exchange rate
    • A value too low would cause a shortage of funds in the market (deficit)
    • A value too high would cause a surplus of funds in the market (surplus)

Managed

Definition

  • Where the value of a currency is allowed to fluctuate between an acceptable upper and lower limit in accordance with the market forces of demand and supply in the foreign exchange market
  • The country's central bank will intervene to prevent the currency from moving outside of these acceptable levels
    • Can raise or lower interest rates
    • Or buy and sell large volumes of the currency to increase supply or demand

Sepculating

Against Fixed Exchange Rate

  • Predicting the direction of the movement (revaluation or devaluation) is easy
  • Knowing when the change will be made is much more difficult

Against Floating Exchange Rate

  • You know the exchange rate will change every day
  • Can't easily predict the direction of the movement (appreciation or depreciation)

Factors Affecting

  • Australian dollar is depreciating when looking at mid-term trends
  • Depreciating since 2012
  • Listed below in order of significance

Terms of Trade

  • Recent unfavourable movement in the terms of trade since 2012
  • Especially from 2014 onwards where the world saw a large decrease in the price of commodities
    • Indicated the end of the commodities price boom
  • This caused a large decrease in the export price index
  • Caused a significant unfavourable movement in the terms of trade
  • Due to the inelasticity of our exports, this fall in price did not result in any significant change in volume sold
    • Inelasticity due to the lack of available substitutes for our exports
    • Intrinsic to our exports due to their composition constisting primarily of natural minerals
      • Iron ore, coal, natural gas are our 3 largest exports
  • This decreased our export income
    • Reduced demand for the AUD
    • Caused depreciation
  • This is perhaps the most significant factor causing the depreciation over the last 4 years

World Economic Growth

  • Slowing growth from China over recent years
    • Average growth over the past decade has been 10%
    • Forecasts for 2016 are 6 to 7%
  • Since China is a major trading partner with Australia
    • The number 1 consumer of Australian exports
    • The number 1 country that Australia imports from
  • Decrease demand for Australian exports
    • Decrease demand for AUD
    • Depreciation of AUD
  • In addition, from 2014 onwards
    • There has been a surplus in the supply of base metals, particularly iron ore
      • Due to large infrastruture investment during the mining boom riding on the back of high commodity prices
      • Created large production capacity for commodities
    • Since iron ore is Australia's number 1 export
    • This has dramatically reduced demand for Australia's exports
    • Decrease in demand for AUD
    • Caused depreciation in AUD

Interest Rate Differential

  • Australia currently has a higher inflation rate relative to other countries
  • But we are in an expansionary phase of our monetary policy
    • Reserve bank is in the process of reducing the cash rate
  • This is reducing the return investors are receiving on their investments in Australia
  • Makes investors more likely to shift their holdings to overseas markets
    • As other countries lift their cash rates
  • This increases supply of the Australian dollar in the foreign exchange market, causing a depreciation

International Capital Flows and Foreign Investment

  • The stabilisation of key markets such as the US in 2013
  • Combined with slowing growth in the Australian economy due to the lower growth in important sectors such as mining
  • Caused investors to shift their holdings overseas
    • To more attractive investment destinations
  • Caused decreased direct and portfolio investment in Australia
    • Decrease in demand for AUD
    • Causes a depreciation

Relative Inflation Rate

  • Australia has benefitted from lower relative inflation rates over the previous two decades
    • This increased the competitiveness of our exports
    • As the prices of our exports were lower when compared to countries with higher inflation rates
    • This aided in appreciating our exchange rate during the mining boom
  • Australia's inflation rate was 1.7% as of December 2015
  • Despite this being below the RBA's target inflation rate of between 2 and 3%
  • Most other trade competing nations have had similarly low levels of inflation
    • European Union was 1.2% as of December 2015
  • Hence the inflation rate has not been of major influence over the Australian dollar's depreciation recently

Domestic Economic Growth

  • Australia has a high marginal propensity to import
    • Meaning strong domestic growth directly correlates with higher spending on imports
    • Increases supply of AUD
    • Causes depreciation
  • As of December 2015, Australia's annual GDP growth rate was 3%
    • Higher than expected
  • Despite this, domestic growth is reasonably volatile
  • Does not have a large influence on the exchange rate

Speculation

  • The exchange rate is significantly influenced by speculation on the part of investors in the short term
  • Since 2014, the improved performance of the US economy
  • Combined with the slowing growth of the Australian economy
  • Has seen demand for the AUD decrease as central banks around the world no longer view the AUD as an alternate, stable currency
    • Other markets have better investment opportunities
  • Causes depreciation

Effects

Depreciation

International Competitiveness

  • Increases international competitiveness of Australian exporters
    • Exports become cheaper for foreign consumers
