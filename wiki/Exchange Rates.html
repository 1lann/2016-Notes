Exchange Rates

Definition

  • The price of one country's currency in terms of another country's currency
  • Defined so that transactions can occur between countries

Foreign Exchange Market

  • Market where the currency of other countries is bought and sold

Measurements

Bilateral

Definition

  • The bilateral exchange rate measures the value of one country's currency in terms of another country's currency
  • Also called the cross exchange rate

Example

  • For example, AUD in terms of USD
    • 1 AUD = 0.90 USD
    • 1 USD = 0.71 British pounds

Quoting

  • Most commonly quoted exchange rate is the bilateral exchange rate against USD
  • There are two ways to quote a currency using the bilateral exchange rate:
    • In terms of a country's purchasing power
      • 1 AUD = 0.75 USD
    • In terms of a common currency
      • 1 USD = 1.33 AUD

Trade Weighted Index

Definition

  • The trade weighted index is a measure of movements in the Australian dollar against a weighted average of 20 other currencies
  • The currencies are weighted according to their importance to Australia's international trade flows

Advantages Over Bilateral Measure

  • TWI is more accurate and more significant than the bilateral exchange rate holistically
    • Though the bilateral rate is more significant for an individual travelling to South Africa, for example
  • As it is trade weighted, it relates to changes in performance of Australia's balance of payments over time

Formula

  • Price of good in foreign currency
    • Multiply price of good in domestic currency by the bilateral rate, quoted in terms of the domestic currency (ie. domestic currency is 1)
  • Price of good in domestic currency
    • Multiply price of good in foreign currency by the bilateral rate, quoted in terms of the foreign currency (ie. foreign currency is 1)

Example

  • Price in foreign currency
    • Given a good costs $60 AUD
    • The exchange rate is 1 AUD = 0.75 USD
    • The good costs \(60 \times 0.75 = 45\) USD
  • Price in domestic currency
    • Given a good costs $80 USD
    • The exchange rate is 1 AUD = 0.75 USD
    • The exchange rate in terms of the USD is 1 USD = \(\frac{1}{0.75}\) = 1.33 AUD
    • The good costs \(80 \times 1.33 = 106.4\) AUD

Model

  • The price of a currency is determined by the market forces of supply and demand in the foreign exchange market

Demand

  • In an international transaction where an international consumer wishes to buy an export produced by an Australian producer:
    • The Australian producer will expect to be paid in Australian dollars
    • The international consumer will need to convert their country's currency into Australian dollars
    • They will purchase Australian dollars on the foreign exchange market, creating demand for AUD
    • The purchase of the export is a credit in Australia's BOP
    • Hence the credit in the BOP represents demand for AUD
  • All credits in a country's BOP represent demand for the country's currency

Supply

  • In an international transaction where an Australian consumer wishes to buy an export produced by an international producer in another country:
    • Producer will expect to be paid in their country's currency
    • Australian consumer will convert AUD into the foreign currency to purchase the import
    • Supplies Australian dollars to the market
    • The purchase of an import is a debit in Australia's BOP
    • Hence the debit in the BOP represents supply of AUD
  • All debits in a country's BOP represents supply of the country's currency

Increased Demand for the AUD

  • Increased demand for Australian exports
    • Exports are sold in AUD
    • Overseas consumers require AUD to purchase the exports
    • These consumers must therefore purchase AUD (demand for the dollar)
    • Examples: purchase of iron ore, overseas students studying in Australia, overseas tourists travelling to Australia
  • Increased foreign investment into Australia
    • Foreign investment in Australia requires AUD to perform domestic transactions
    • Overseas investors must convert their money into AUD to perform these transactions
    • Requires the purchase of AUD
    • Examples: construction of new mining infrastructure by an overseas mining company in Australia
  • Increased income receipts for Australians working overseas
    • Australians are paid in AUD
    • Companies hiring Australians must convert the income for these employees into AUD
    • Requires the purchase of AUD
  • Increased dividend payments to Australians who have invested in overseas companies
    • Dividend payments must be converted to Australian dollars before being paid to Australian investors
    • Requires purchasing AUD
  • Increased profit flows to Australian companies with operations overseas
    • Profit flows are converted to Australian dollars before being returned to multinational companies with their headquaters in Australia

Increased Supply of the AUD

  • Increased spending on imports
    • Imports in other countries are sold in that country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency
    • This is the sale of AUD in the foreign exchange market, suppling AUD to the market
    • Examples: Purchase of cars from Japan, Australian tourists travelling overseas, Australian students studying in overseas universities
  • Increased foreign investment in other countries
    • Transactions in foreign countries that arise from investment in those countries take place in the foreign country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency
    • Examples: an Australian company building a manufacturing plant in China
  • Increased income payments to overseas residents working in Australia
    • Overseas residents are paid in their country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD to foreign currency
  • Increased dividend payments to overseas investors who have invested in Australian companies
    • Overseas investors expect to be paid in their country's currency
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency
  • Increased profit flows to overseas companies with operations in Australia
    • Overseas companies will convert profit made from operations in Australia into the currency of the country the company's headquarters are in
    • Requires converting AUD into foreign currency

Movements

Appreciation

  • Where one unit of a currency is able to buy more units of another currency than it previously could

Depreciation

  • Where one unit of a currency is only able to buy less units of another currency than it previously could

Types

Floating

Definition

  • Where the market forces of supply and demand alone in the foreign exchange market determine the price of a country's currency
  • Referred to colloquially as a "clean" float

Advantages

  • Currency can act as a shock absorber for economic shocks to the country
  • Makes monetary policy more effective
  • Will aid in reducing volatility in the current account balance

Australia

  • Australia floated our currency in December 1983

Fixed

Definition

  • Where the price of a country's currency is set at a particular value and maintained by the country's central bank
  • Central bank may change the value they set the currency at
    • Increasing the value of the currency is called a revaluation
    • Decreasing the value of the currency is called a devaluation

Disadvantages

  • Requires repeated intervention by the central bank to adjust the exchange rate
    • A value too low would cause a shortage of funds in the market (deficit)
    • A value too high would cause a surplus of funds in the market (surplus)

Managed

Definition

  • Where the value of a currency is allowed to fluctuate between an acceptable upper and lower limit in accordance with the market forces of demand and supply in the foreign exchange market
  • The country's central bank will intervene to prevent the currency from moving outside of these acceptable levels
    • Can raise or lower interest rates
    • Or buy and sell large volumes of the currency to increase supply or demand
  • Most countries use this system, albeit with a fairly large acceptable range
    • Referred to colloquially as a "dirty" float

Raising the Exchange Rate

  • To raise the exchange rate because it is reaching too low levels, the reserve bank could:
    • Use its reserves of foreign currency to purchase a large amount of Australian dollars
      • Increases demand, causing an appreciation
    • Increase interest rates
      • Increases foreign investment into Australia, increasing demand for the Australian dollar
      • Causes an appreciation

Sepculating

Against Fixed Exchange Rate

  • Predicting the direction of the movement (revaluation or devaluation) is easy
  • Knowing when the change will be made is much more difficult

Against Floating Exchange Rate

  • You know the exchange rate will change every day
  • Can't easily predict the direction of the movement (appreciation or depreciation)

Balance of Payments

  • A floating exchange rate causes a country's balance of payments to balance
    • Current account and capital and financial account sum to 0
  • Exchange rate varies to change the price of transactions in the balance of payments
  • For example:
    • If there is an excess supply of AUD because of Australians purchasing a large number of imports
    • Will cause a depreciation in the AUD
    • Raises the price of imports, decreasing demand for them

Factors Affecting

  • Australian dollar is depreciating when looking at mid-term trends
  • Depreciating since 2012
  • Factors listed below in order of significance

Terms of Trade

  • Recent unfavourable movement in the terms of trade since 2012
  • Especially from 2014 onwards where the world saw a large decrease in the price of commodities
    • Indicated the end of the commodities price boom
  • This caused a large decrease in the export price index
  • Caused a significant unfavourable movement in the terms of trade
  • Due to the inelasticity of our exports, this fall in price did not result in any significant change in volume sold
    • Inelasticity due to the lack of available substitutes for our exports
    • Intrinsic to our exports due to their composition constisting primarily of natural minerals
      • Iron ore, coal, natural gas are our 3 largest exports
  • This decreased our export income
    • Reduced demand for the AUD
    • Caused depreciation
  • This is perhaps the most significant factor causing the depreciation over the last 4 years

World Economic Growth

  • Slowing growth from China over recent years
    • Average growth over the past decade has been 10%
    • Forecasts for 2016 are 6 to 7%
  • Since China is a major trading partner with Australia
    • The number 1 consumer of Australian exports
    • The number 1 country that Australia imports from
  • Decrease demand for Australian exports
    • Decrease demand for AUD
    • Depreciation of AUD
  • In addition, from 2014 onwards
    • There has been a surplus in the supply of base metals, particularly iron ore
      • Due to large infrastruture investment during the mining boom riding on the back of high commodity prices
      • Created large production capacity for commodities
    • Since iron ore is Australia's number 1 export
    • This has dramatically reduced demand for Australia's exports
    • Decrease in demand for AUD
    • Caused depreciation in AUD

Interest Rate Differential

  • Australia currently has a higher interest rate relative to other countries
  • But we are in an expansionary phase of our monetary policy
    • Reserve bank is in the process of reducing the cash rate
  • This is reducing the return investors are receiving on their investments in Australia
  • Makes investors more likely to shift their holdings to overseas markets
    • As other countries lift their cash rates
  • This increases supply of the Australian dollar in the foreign exchange market, causing a depreciation

International Capital Flows and Foreign Investment

  • The stabilisation of key markets such as the US in 2013
  • Combined with slowing growth in the Australian economy due to the lower growth in important sectors such as mining
  • Caused investors to shift their holdings overseas
    • To more attractive investment destinations
  • Caused decreased direct and portfolio investment in Australia
    • Decrease in demand for AUD
    • Causes a depreciation
  • Australian investors may also shift their investments overseas, rather than keep their holdings in Australia
    • Supply Australian dollars to the market
    • Compound depreciation

Relative Inflation Rate

  • Australia has benefitted from lower relative inflation rates over the previous two decades
    • This increased the competitiveness of our exports
    • As the prices of our exports were lower when compared to countries with higher inflation rates
    • This aided in appreciating our exchange rate during the mining boom
  • Australia's inflation rate was 1.7% as of December 2015
  • Despite this being below the RBA's target inflation rate of between 2 and 3%
  • Most other trade competing nations have had similarly low levels of inflation
    • European Union was 1.2% as of December 2015
  • Hence the inflation rate has not been of major influence over the Australian dollar's depreciation recently

Domestic Economic Growth

  • Australia has a high marginal propensity to import
    • Meaning strong domestic growth directly correlates with higher spending on imports
    • Increases supply of AUD
    • Causes depreciation
  • As of December 2015, Australia's annual GDP growth rate was 3%
    • Higher than expected
  • Despite this, domestic growth is reasonably volatile
  • Does not have a large influence on the exchange rate

Speculation

  • The exchange rate is significantly influenced by speculation on the part of investors in the short term
    • Daily turnover according to the RBA is $180 billion
    • Eighth largest market in the world
  • Since 2013, the improved performance of the US economy
  • Combined with the slowing growth of the Australian economy
  • Has seen demand for the AUD decrease as central banks around the world no longer view the AUD as an alternate, stable currency
    • Other markets have better investment opportunities
  • Causes depreciation

Effects

Exports

  • As dollar depreciates, international competitiveness of exporters improve
    • Exports become cheaper for international consumers to buy
  • Improvement in growth in export industries
  • Can cause structural change in economy
    • For example, the conclusion of the mining boom (commodities price and TOT boom) in 2012
    • Causing depreciation in Australian dollar
    • Impacts positively on other manufacturing and service providing industries
  • For exporters whose contracts are denominated in foreign currency rather than Australian dollars
    • Rather than changing the price of their exports
    • A depreciation increases their revenue when converted back into Australian dollars
  • May see the beginnings of the effects of Dutch disease
    • Where displaced workers from the declining mining sector add to unemployment
  • Depreciation is generally good for exporters
    • May be harmful if they must import intermediate goods
    • Depreciation will cause these to cost more
    • May eliminate any additional profit incurred from the depreciation

Imports

  • As the dollar depreciates
  • Imports become more expensive for Australian consumers
  • Increases the competitiveness of import-competing industries
  • Encourages substitution of imported products with locally made goods
  • Decreases import spending
  • For example, the tourism and education sectors
    • People choosing to travel and study in Australia rather than overseas because it's cheaper

Balance of Goods and Services

  • Improvement in international competitiveness
  • Increase in export income
    • After a time lag
    • As predicted by the J curve theory (??)
  • Decrease in import spending
  • Balance of goods and services is expected to improve
    • Move towards a surplus in the long term

Primary Income Category of Current Account

  • Depreciation makes foreign investment in Australia cheaper
    • Australian assets are cheaper to purchase for overseas investors
  • Greater foreign direct and portfolio investment will increase dividend payments and profit flows out of Australia
    • In addition, since a significant portion of foreign debt (between 40 and 60%) is denominated in foreign currency
    • Increases size of interest payments
    • Known as the valuation effect
  • Thus increases deficit in primary income category
  • This effect is pretty weak, as there are a lot of other factors that affect this deficit

Current Account Deficit

  • Balance of goods and services is expected to improve
  • Primary income deficit is expect to increase
  • Since goods and services is roughly 4 times as large as primary income in dollar value terms, and given the inelastic nature of Australia's exports and imports
  • Results in the CAD decreasing

Foreign Debt

  • As a significant portion of Australia's foreign debt is denominated in foreign currency
    • Between 40 and 60%
    • Mainly USD, Yen, Euro, and British Pound
  • The instant the AUD depreciates, there is an increase in the size of foreign debt in AUD terms
    • Referred to as the valuation effect

Inflation

  • Depreciation leads to increased price of imports
  • This causes tradables inflation to increase
  • Given tradables represent a signficant proportion of CPI
  • This could have a considerable impact on the inflation rate
  • In addition, increased input costs for domestic producers
    • Due to higher prices of imported intermediate goods
    • Can also increase cost push inflation
  • Increase in net exports and any multiplier effects can increase the price of non tradables and thus demand pull inflation

Economic Growth

  • Improvement in export industry
  • Improvement in domestic import competing industries
  • Causes increase in economic growth
  • In addition to declining import spending improving aggregate demand
    • Seen most in declining outbound tourist numbers
  • Since 2013 the declining mining sector has reduced this economic growth
  • Can result in a two speed economy
    • Where one sector is growing rapidly, while others are slowing

Employment

  • With increased economic growth, should cause a derived increase in demand for labour as output increases
  • Decreases cyclical unemployment
  • Increase in aggregate demand can cause a rise in output, decreasing levels of under employment
  • Mining boom also caused structural unemployment
    • Massive appreciation of dollar
    • A factor that played a part in causing Ford to cease Australian operations in 2016, Holden and Toyota in 2017

Historical Data

Mining Boom

Effect

  • Massive appreciation between 2001 - 2008

Cause

  • Increasing demand for raw minerals on the back of large growth in countries with economies based heavily around manufacturing (China)
  • Caused commodities price boom
  • Since Australia's top exports are such commodities (Iron Ore top, 20.2% of all goods and services exports as at June 2015)
  • Increased demand for Australian exports
  • Due to large price boom, increased export income
  • Increased demand for AUD
  • Caused appreciation

GFC

Effect

  • Massive depreciation between 2008 - 2009

Second Mining Boom

Effect

  • Massive appreciation between 2009 - 2011
  • Returned to levels above those of before the GFC

Slowing Growth in China

Effect

  • Depreciation since 2012

Effects

Question Explain the likely effects of this depreciation on the Australian economy

Essay

  • Exports
    • Depreciation reduces price of exports for international consumers
    • Increases international competitiveness
    • After a time lag, this can result in the increase in sales of exports due to the competitive advantage from a lower dollar
      • J curve theory
    • If the Australian producer imports intermediate goods in the production of the final consumption good
      • The depreciation will cause these imports to be more expensive
      • Increases costs for the business
      • May eliminate any profit incurred from a lower dollar
    • Can cause structural change in the economy
      • Conclusion of mining boom over recent years has seen the Australian economy begin shifting towards the manufacturing and services sectors
      • Away from the mining sectors
      • Depreciation impacts positively on these other sectors
  • Imports
    • Depreciation increases the price of imports for Australian consumers in Australian dollar terms
    • Decreases the demand for imports as they become more expensive
    • Decreases import spending
    • Bestows a competitive advantage on import competing industries
      • Become comparatively cheaper
      • Increases growth in these industries
    • For example, tourism
      • Declining outbound tourist numbers as people chose to travel in Australia instead
  • BOGS
    • Increase in export income (credits)
    • Decrease in import spending (debits)
    • BOGS should move towards a surplus
    • After a time lag from J curve
  • Foreign Debt
    • Valuation effect
    • Denominated in foreign currencies
    • ...
  • Primary income
    • Since a significant portion of Australia's foreign debt is denominated in foreign currency (40 - 60%)
    • Depreciation immediately increases the size of this foreign debt in Australian dollar terms
    • Increases the size of the interest repayments that must be paid
    • These interest payments are recorded as debits in the primary income category of the current account
    • Increases the size of the deficit in the primary income account
  • Current Account Deficit
    • The goods and services category is roughly 4 times the size of the primary income category
    • The effect of the exchange rate on the primary income is also relatively weak, as there are many other factors that also influence it
    • This means that the CAD is likely to trend towards a surplus with the improvement in the BOGS
  • Domestic growth
    • Lower import spending
    • Higher export income
    • Growth in import-competing industries
    • Larger aggregate expenditure
    • Two speed economy

  • Inflation

    • Will increase demand pull inflation
    • In addition, higher import prices for intermediate goods will cost producers more money
      • Cost push inflation
  • Unemployment
    • Increased domestic growth
    • Increase demand for labour
    • Cyclical unemployment should decrease
    • Structural changes in the economy can also cause structural unemployment
