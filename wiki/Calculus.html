Differentiation

Polynomials

Rule

\[ \begin{align*} y & = ax^n \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = anx^{n - 1} \\ \end{align*} \]

Common Derivatives

  • \(y = x^2\), \(y' = 2x\)
  • \(y = \frac{a}{x}\), \(y' = -\frac{a}{x^2}\)
  • \(y = \frac{a}{x^n}\), \(y' = -\frac{na}{x^{n + 1}}\)
  • \(y = a\sqrt{x}\), \(y' = \frac{a}{2\sqrt{x}}\)

Product Rule

\[ \begin{align*} y & = uv \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = \frac{du}{dx} v + \frac{dv}{dx} u \\ \end{align*} \]

Quotient Rule

\[ \begin{align*} y & = \frac{u}{v} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = \frac{\frac{du}{dx} v - \frac{dv}{dx} u}{v^2} \\ \end{align*} \]

Chain Rule

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \frac{du}{dx} \]

Trigonometry

Sine

\[ \begin{align*} y & = \sin{x} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = \cos{x} \\ \end{align*} \]

Cosine

\[ \begin{align*} y & = \cos{x} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = -\sin{x} \\ \end{align*} \]

Tangent

\[ \begin{align*} y & = \tan{x} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = \frac{1}{\cos^2{x}} \\ & = \sec^2{x} \\ \end{align*} \]

e

\[ \begin{align*} y & = e^u \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = \frac{du}{dx} e^u \\ \end{align*} \]

Global Maximum in Domain

  • Find the global maximum of a function within a specific domain
  • Solve \(f'(x) = 0\) for turning points
  • Find \(f(x)\) for turning points and minimum and maximum value in domain
  • Select maximum or minimum point

Points of Interest

Types

  • Turning points
    • Minimum (concave up)
    • Maximum (concave down)
  • Inflection points
    • Horizontal
    • Vertical

Nature

  • For a turning point:
    • Whether it is minimum or maximum
  • For an inflection point:
    • Whether it is a horizontal or vertical inflection point
    • Whether it is on a rising or falling curve

Stationary Points

Definition

  • Where the first derivative is equal to zero
  • Includes:
    • Minimum turning points
    • Maximum turning points
    • Horizontal points of inflection

Concavity

Definition

  • Whether a curve is concave up or concave down at a point
  • Determined by sign of second derivative
  • If \(f''(x) > 0\):
    • Concave up
  • If \(f''(x) < 0\):
    • Concave down

Turning Points

Definition

  • Where the first derivative is equal to zero
  • Local maxima/minima
  • No change in concavity around point

Locating

  • Solve \(f'(x) = 0\)

Determining Nature

Sign Test

  • Find sign of \(f'(x - a)\) and \(f'(x + a)\) for some small \(a\)
  • If positive then negative
    • Maximum turning point
  • If negative then positive
    • Minimum turning point
  • If positive then positive
    • Horizontal inflection point on rising curve
  • If negative then negative
    • Horizontal inflection point on falling curve

Second Derivative Test

  • If \(f''(x) > 0\):
    • Minimum turning point
  • If \(f''(x) < 0\):
    • Maximum turning point
  • If \(f''(x) = 0\):
    • Inconclusive
    • Use the sign test

Inflection Points

Definition

  • Occur when second derivative is zero and there is a change in concavity

Horizontal Inflection Point

  • Where first derivative is also zero

Locating

All Inflection Points

  • Solve \(f''(x) = 0\)
  • Check concavity around each point
    • Check sign of \(f''(x - a)\) and \(f''(x + a)\)
    • Ensure signs are different

Horizontal Inflection Points

  • Solve \(f'(x) = 0\)
  • Check \(f''(x) = 0\)
  • Check concavity changes around each point

Graphing Derivatives

  • Sketch a graph of the first and second derivative from a graph of \(f(x)\)
  • First derivative graph will be a polynomial of order one less than that of \(f(x)\)
  • Second derivative graph will be polynomial of order two less than that of \(f(x)\)
  • Technique:
    • Map out known points
    • Determine appearance of graph around known points
    • Imagine general shape of polynomial (both positive and negative variants)
    • Join up known points

Features

First Derivative Graph

  • Passes through x axis for turning points and horizontal inflections on \(f(x)\)
  • Above x axis for positive gradients in \(f(x)\)
  • Below x axis for negative gradients in \(f(x)\)
  • Minimum/maximum turning points at points of steepest gradient on \(f(x)\)

Second Derivative Graph

  • Above x axis for concave up parts of \(f(x)\)
  • Below x axis for concave down parts of \(f(x)\)
  • Passes through zero for inflection points

Integration

Given the function:

\[ f(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} \]

The integral of this function is \(y\):

\[ \int f(x) dx = y + c \]

Where \(c\) is a real number.

Properties

\[ \begin{align*} \int k f(x) dx & = k \int f(x) dx \int (f(x) \pm g(x)) dx & = \int f(x) dx \pm \int g(x) dx \\ \int (f(x) g(x)) dx & = \int f(x) dx \int g(x) dx \\ \int (\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}) dx & = \frac{\int f(x) dx}{\int g(x) dx} \\ \end{align*} \]

Polynomial Functions

Given the function:

\[ f(x) = ax^n \]

The indefinite integral is:

\[ \int f(x) dx = \frac{ax^{n + 1}}{n + 1} + c \]

Procedure

  • Pick the main term (with the exponent) and ignore the coefficient
  • Increase the power by 1
  • Differentiate and observe the coefficient of this derivative
  • Manipulate the original term with its increased exponent so that its coefficient when differentiated will produce the same coefficient as in the original function
    • Divide the original coefficient by the derivative's coefficient

Examples

\[ \begin{align*} \int 2x + 3x^2 dx & = x^2 + x^3 + c \\ \int x^2 + x^4 dx & = \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{x^5}{5} + c \\ \int \frac{3}{x^2} dx & = \frac{-3}{x} + c \\ \end{align*} \]

With Chain Rule

\[ \begin{align*} \int 12x(x^2 + 3)^2 & = 2(x^2 + 3)^3 + c \\ \int (6x^2 + 8x)(x^3 + 2x^2 + 4)^2 & = \frac{2}{3}(x^3 + 2x^2 + 4)^3 \\ \end{align*} \]

Expanding Brackets

  • If we're asked to integrate a composite function (eg. \(12x(x^2 + 3)^4\))
  • The coefficient out the front of the brackets must be a scalar multiple of the derivative of the function inside the brackets
  • Otherwise we have to expand the brackets and integrate each term separately
  • For example:
    • \(12x(x^2 + 3)^4\) is doable
    • \((x^2 + 23)(x - 10)\) is not

Exponential Functions

Given the function:

\[ f(x) = ae^{g(x)} \]

The indefinite integral is:

\[ \int f(x) dx = \frac{a}{g'(x)} e^{g(x)} + c \]

Examples

\[ \begin{align*} \int e^{2x} dx & = \frac{1}{2} e^{2x} + c \\ \int 4e^{3x + 1} dx & = \frac{4}{3} e^{3x + 1} + c \\ \end{align*} \]

Trigonometric Functions

The indefinite integrals of the trigonometric functions are (\(\tan{x}\) isn't required):

\[ \begin{align*} \int \sin{x} dx & = -\cos{x} + c \\ \int \cos{x} dx & = \sin{x} + c \\ \end{align*} \]

Examples

\[ \begin{align*} \int \sin{2x + 1} dx & = -\frac{1}{2}\cos{2x + 1} + c \\ \int 3\cos{5x} dx & = \frac{3}{5}\sin{5x} + c \\ \end{align*} \]

Common Integrals

\[ \begin{align*} \int x^n dx & = \frac{x^{n + 1}}{n + 1} \\ \int \frac{1}{x^n} dx & = -\frac{n - 1}{x^{n - 1}} \\ \int e^x dx & = e^x \\ \end{align*} \]

Definite Integrals

  • The definite integral of a function finds the area under a curve between two points
\[ \int^b_a f'(x) dx = f(b) - f(a) \\ \]

Inscribed and Circumscribed Area

  • Value of an integral can be approximated by averaging the inscribed and circumscribed area
  • Inscribed area is the area below the curve approximated with rectangles that do not extend above the curve
  • Circumscribed area is area below the curve approximated with rectangles that extend above the curve
  • Value will be between inscribed and circumscribed area

Negative Area

  • Area below the x axis is negative, and above is positive
  • If we're finding the area between two points, and the graph goes below the x axis
    • Then the portion under the x axis will cancel out with the portion above
    • Since it acts as a negative value
  • To find the true area between a curve and the x axis
    • Either split the function up into sections where it intersects with the x axis and negate the sections below the x axis
    • Or find the definite integral of the absolute value of the function

Area Between Curves

  • Find the intersection between the curves
    • Equate the two equations and solve for \(x\)
  • Area between the two curves is the area under the top curve subtract the area under the bottom one:
\[ \int^b_a f(x) - \int^b_a g(x) \]
  • Same equation applies even if part of the curve lies below the x axis
  • If the upper graph becomes the lower graph between two intersections
    • Split up by finding the intersections between the two functions
    • Split the integral into multiple components and negate the opposing parts

Find Integral from Derivative

  • Given the derivative for something involving product or quotient rule
  • Asks to find the integration of a term of the above derivative
  • Must write the integral of the derivative equals the original function
  • Split the integral up over the terms of the derivative
  • Isolate the integral of the desired term
  • Solve the rest of the integrals

Rectilinear Motion

  • Relationship between position/displacement, velocity, and acceleration
  • Find velocity from displacement, or acceleration from velocity, by differentiating with respect to time
  • Find displacement from velocity, or velocity from acceleration, by integrating with respect to time

Components

  • Displacement (\(s\)) or position (\(x\))
    • Integrate velocity
  • Velocity (\(v\))
    • Integrate acceleration
    • Differentiate displacement
  • Acceleration (\(a\))
    • Differentiate velocity

Terminology

  • At rest
    • \(v = 0\)
  • At origin
    • \(x = 0\)
  • Constant acceleration
    • \(a = k, k \in \mathbb{R}\)
  • Initially, ...
    • \(t = 0\)

Speed and Velocity

  • Velocity is a vector quantity and requires a direction
    • Direction given in the form of a positive or negative value
  • Speed is a scalar quantity and should always be positive
    • Take the absolute value of the velocity

Distance and Displacement

  • Displacement is the straight line distance between two points
    • Calculated by \(s(x_1) - s(x_2)\)
    • Take the definite integral between the two points
      • Ie. \(\int_{x_1}^{x_2} s(x) dx\)
  • Distance is the distance travelled by the object in moving between two points
    • Find turning points of the graph and add up the distance between them and the end points
    • Or take the definite integral of the absolute value of the function
      • Ie. \(\int_{x_1}^{x_2} |s(x)| dx\)

Optimisation

  • Find optimimum value for an expression
  • Differentiate and solve for turning points
  • Find minimum or maximum value

Two Variables

  • Write the equation to optimise in terms of both variables
  • Write an equation relating the two variables
  • Rearrange for one of the variables
  • Substitute into the equation to optimise
  • Solve for optimimum value
  • Solve for second variable using relating equation

Rates of Change

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx} & \approx \frac{\delta y}{\delta x} \\ \delta y & \approx \delta x \frac{dy}{dx} \\ \end{align*} \]
  • Be aware we can also use the inverse of the derivative \(\frac{dx}{dy}\)

Percentage Change

  • What is the approximate percentage change in \(y\) when \(x\) changes by \(k\) percent?
  • Given \(\frac{\delta x}{x}\)
  • Asked for \(\frac{\delta y}{y}\)
\[ \begin{align*} \frac{\delta x}{x} & = \frac{k}{100} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & \approx \frac{\delta y}{\delta x} \\ \delta y & \approx \frac{dy}{dx} \delta x \\ \frac{\delta y}{y} & \approx \frac{dy \delta x}{dx y} \\ \end{align*} \]
  • This will simplify to some constant multiplied by \(\frac{\delta x}{x}\)

Marginal Rates of Change

Economic Functions

  • Cost function, \(C(x)\)
  • Revenue function, \(R(x)\)
  • Profit function, \(P(x) = R(x) - C(x)\)

Marginal Functions

  • Marginal cost gives the cost of producing the next item at the stage of production where we're currently producing \(x\)
  • Marginal cost is \(C'(x)\)
  • Marginal cost of \(x\)th item is \(C'(x - 1)\)
  • Same for revenue and profit

Total Rate of Change

  • Given the derivative function
  • Asked to find the total change in a function between two values
    • Find the definite integral between the two values
  • Find total change in (for example) the 4th second, or the 3rd week
    • Find definite integral between \(n - 1\) and \(n\)
