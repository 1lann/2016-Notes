Globalisation

Definition

  • Nobel prize in economics Joseph Stiglitz
  • The closer integration of countries and people bought about by the reduction in cost of transport and communication, and the breaking down of artificial barriers to the flow of goods, services, capital, knowledge, and people across borders
  • Currently in 3rd wave of globalisation
    • Started in 1980s

Causes

Technology

  • Decreased cost of transportation
    • Standardisation of transportation systems
    • Through use of shipping containers
  • Increased speed of transportation
    • Increased use of airplanes due to improvements in technology
    • Enabled faster deliveries
  • Increased ease of international communication
    • Eg. email, instant messenger applications
    • Allows for coordination of international operations
    • Allows for constant access to markets across all world timezones

Government Policy

  • Opening of domestic markets to international producers
    • Through unilateral trade reform
  • Reduced governmental assistance to firms
    • Reduction in "protection"
    • More level playing field for international producers
    • Improves efficiency in producing goods and services as businesses are forced to compete

Organisations

  • Organisations encouraging reduction in protection through:
    • Encouraging unilateral trade reform
    • Facilitating bilateral and multilateral free trade agreements
  • Increase in free trade agreements could cause trade diversion rather than trade creation

Multinational Corporations

  • Driven globalisation in an attempt to increase profit by reducing costs
  • Evident through the processes of:
    • Outsourcing
      • Purchasing a good or service from another firm so we don't have to manufacture/supply it ourselves
    • Offshoring
      • Moving operations to another country to reduce costs
      • Usually to reduce labour costs in manufacturing
  • Have established global brands, produced by global manufacturing processes, distributed by global networks

Media

  • Global media networks transfer information and knowledge of other cultures to consumers in many countries
  • Increases global awareness of popular culture
    • Reinforced by tourism to other countries
  • Influences consumers to want what the rest of the world has
    • Creates demand for international products
  • Enhances tolerance of other cultures (eg. tolerance for people of different races and religions)
  • Decreases effects of cultural barriers (eg. language and customs)

Effects

Positive

  • Access to wider variety of higher quality goods and service
    • Due to:
      • Increased imports from foreign countries
  • Lowers prices
    • Decreases country's inflation rate
    • Increases consumers' real income
    • Increases consumer purchasing power
    • Due to:
      • Increased competition from international markets forcing firms to remain competitive
      • Reduced cost of production due to cheaper international supply chains
  • Provides more, better paying jobs
    • Increases real income
    • Increases standard of living
  • Increases competition
    • Increases efficiency and productivity
    • Advances technology
  • Reduces poverty
    • Creates jobs, increasing real income
    • Increased foreign direct investment in developing countries creates economic growth, raising the standard of living
  • Increases economic growth
    • Increased trade, job opportunities
    • Decreased prices
  • Increases living standards
    • Increased real income of consumers
  • International transfer of technology and knowledge
    • International labour force spreading skills
    • Multinational companies bringing their technology into new countries
  • Increases multiculturalism
    • International labour force introducing variety of cultures to the rest of the world
  • Promotes world peace
    • War causes massive global economic destabilisation
    • As economies are interdependent, shocks propagate faster and are more harmful

Negative

  • Increases unemployment amongst low skill workers
    • Firms attempt to reduce costs by replacing low skill workers with improved technology
  • Increases structural unemployment due to rate of structural change
    • Rapid changes in global demand for goods and services
    • Causes firms to shift operations quickly (high rate of structural change) to satisfy demand
    • Skill sets of workers become mismatched against the skill sets required by firms
    • Increases unemployment
  • Entrenches exploitation of child labour
    • Firms attempt to reduce costs
  • Decreases wages of low skill workers
    • Low skilled workers easily replacable
    • Firms attempt to reduce costs by lowering wages and replacing workers if needed
  • Unfair to developing countries
    • Unable to compete effectively with large scale multinational corporations
    • Fail to produce competitive exports
    • Limits economic growth
  • Volatile capital flow
    • Capital shifts between sectors quickly to accomodate changes in global demand
    • Can destabilise developing economies
    • EXPLAIN MORE??
  • Economic shocks are contagious
    • Economic shocks propagate faster, cause more harm
    • Due to interdependent economies
    • Have severe destabilising effects (eg. GFC in 2008)
  • Increased environmental damage and resource depletion
    • Due to increased industrialisation
  • Erosion of political sovereignty
    • Large corporations gaining political influence because of their large economic influence
    • Create incentive for politicians to favour corporations over consumers when making political decisions to maintain economic prosperity

  • Foreign direct investment

Economic Integration Types

Free trade area

  • Two or more countries
  • Agree to no trade barriers between member countries

Customs union

  • Free trade agreement between member countries
  • Common restrictions and trade barriers placed on imports from all non-member countries

Common market

  • Customs union
  • Common restrictions apply to everything (eg. foreign investment, immigrants)

Monetary union

  • All countries use a single currency
    • Eg. European Union with the Euro
  • Or agree to fixed exchange rates between currencies of member countries

Organisations

Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD)

  • 34 members
  • Undertake research and reviews to advance own economic wellbeing
  • Members must be considered advanced economy
    • Established strong economic base
    • At least $30,000 average annual per capita income

World Trade Organisation (WTO)

  • 188 members
  • Attempt to achieve greater economic integration
  • Administer trade agreements
  • Forum for countries to:
    • Facilitate opening up borders to international trade
    • Negotiate trade agreements
    • Settle trade disputes

International Monetary Fund (IMF)

  • Attempt to ensure stability of global financial systems
  • By ensuring financial stability of individual countries
    • Stability of exchange, interest, and inflation rates

World Bank

  • Provides funding for less developed economies
  • Through low or no interest rate loans
  • For infrastructure development projects
  • Since these countries have little access to international credit markets

Trade

Definition

  • The import and export of goods and services between countries

Inflows

  1. Imports
  2. Foreign tourists travelling to Australia
  3. Immigrants
  4. Foreign investment in Australia

Outflows

  1. Exports
  2. Australian tourists travelling overseas
  3. Emigrants
  4. Foreign investment in other countries

Factors Affecting Trade

Exchange Rate

  • Lower exchange rate
    • Cheaper exports relative to consumer currency
      • Increased demand for exports
      • Increased exports
    • More expensive imports
      • Decreased demand for imports
      • Decreased imports

World Economic Growth

  • Increased world economic growth
    • Increases real income of international consumers
    • Increases demand for exports
    • Increases exports and imports

Domestic Economic Growth

  • Increased domestic growth
    • Increases real income of domestic consumers
    • Increases demand for imports
    • Increases imports

Relative Inflation Rate

  • Relative to trading partners
  • Lower relative inflation rate
    • Decreases price of exports relative to those of other countries
    • Increased exports

Relative Interest Rate

  • Relative to trading partners
  • Higher relative interest rate
    • Increases flow of financial capital into a country

Productivity

  • Higher productivity
    • Increases output for a given input input
    • Decreases average cost
    • Decreases price of exports
    • Increases competitiveness and demand for exports
    • Increases exports

Effects

Consumers

  • Increased variety, quantity, and quality of goods and services
  • Decreased prices
    • More competition from international producers
    • International supply chains reduce costs
  • Increased standard of living
    • More, better paying jobs
    • Increased real income
    • Increased economic growth
    • Increased standard of living

Producers

  • Increased competition
  • Increased production volume
    • Larger market (market is accessible to more consumers)
    • More demand
  • Increased revenue
    • Larger market
    • Increased demand
    • Increased sales
    • Increased revenue

Corporations

Multinational Corporation

  • Headquarters in 1 country
  • Operations (eg. subsidiaries) in many others

Transnational Corporation

  • No readily identifiable headquarters
  • Operations (eg. subsidiaries) in many others

Agreement Types

Unilateral

  • Remove trade barriers for all other countries
  • Don't expect other countries to reciprocate

Bilateral

  • Agreement between 2 countries
  • Removal of trade barriers between them

Multilateral

  • Agreement between more than 2 countries
  • Removal of trade barriers between members

Bilateral vs. Multilateral

  • Advantages of multilateral
    • Increase trade more than a bilateral agreement would
  • Disadvantages of multilateral
    • Significantly harder to negotiate than bilateral agreements as all member countries must be satisfied

Strategic Trade Theory

  • Government recognises challenges faced by smaller firms selling in international markets
    • Not all countries abide by international best practice
    • Creates an uneven playing field, disadvantaging international firms
  • Offers financial or regulatory assistance
  • Increases their competitiveness
    • Increases sales
    • Increases revenue and profit
    • Benefits the country's economy
  • Offered to smaller firms rather than large corporations (eg. not BHP)

Trade Intensity

Formula

$$ \mbox{Trade intensity} = \frac{\mbox{X} + \mbox{M}}{\mbox{GDP}} \times 100 $$

Definition

  • Value of a country's imports and exports as a percentage of its GDP
  • Indication of the openness of a country to international trade
    • Highlights the impact the overseas sector has on the Australian economy
  • Australian statistics
    • Increased significantly
      • 13% in 1970
      • 21% in 2013

Trade Specialisation

  • Where a country dedicates its limited resources to producing goods and services that it is efficient at producing
    • Export the surplus
  • Imports goods and services that it is less efficient at producing, rather than producing them itself

Trade Composition

Definition

  • What a country trades

Comparative Advantage

  • When a country can produce a good or service at a relatively lower cost than another
    • Theoretically measured through opportunity cost
    • When the opportunity cost of a country producing one good over another is less than that of another country
  • In reality, demonstrated through a lower price or superior quality
  • Eg. Australia's mining and agricultural sectors

  • Primary (64%)
    • Minerals (29%)
    • Fuels (21%)
    • Rural (11%)
  • Services (17%)
  • Manufacturing (13%)
    • ETMs (9%)
    • STMs (4%)
  • Other goods, including gold (6%)

Commodity

  • Raw natural material or agricultural product

Primary Exports

  • Natural resources, eg. agriculture, fishing, forestry, mining
  • Rural exports
    • Eg. agriculture, fishing, livestock
  • Non-rural exports
    • Mining
    • Eg. ores, fuels, metals

Manufacturing

  • Simply transformed manufactures (STMs)
    • Commodities that have had very little processing or refining
    • Eg. steel
  • Elaborately transformed manufactures (ETMs)
    • Commodities that have undergone a long or complex process to arrive at the end product
    • Eg. cars

Intermediate Goods

  • Goods used in the production of another final good
  • Not necessarily sold to consumers, but to other manufacturers for use in the production of a final good
  • Eg. steel for manufacturing cars

Consumption Goods

  • Goods sold to the consumer as a final product
  • Eg. a car

Capital Goods

  • Goods used to produce other goods
  • Eg. machinery used in producing a car

Trade Direction

Definition

  • Who we trade with

Trade Partners

  • Trade in Asia and countries around the Pacific Rim accounts for about 80% of Australian exports and 65% of imports
  • Important Countries
    • China
    • United States
    • Japan

Shift in Direction

Shift

  • Shift in trade over Australian history
  • From Britain
    • Originally established due to historic ties (convicts)
  • To Asia and Pacific Rim countries

Reasons

  • Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) free trade agreement
    • Reduced trade barriers with Asia and Pacific Rim
  • Geographic proximity
    • Reduces transport costs
  • Britain joined the European Union
    • Forced common trade barriers on Australia
    • Forced Australia to find new markets
  • Large Asian population
    • Asia have limited supply of commodities relative to their size

Competitiveness

Definition

  • The degree to which a country can produce goods and services that meet the test of international markets, while simultaneously maintaining and expanding the real income of its people in the long term
  • Test of international markets
    • Price
      • Cheaper products are more competitive
      • More important for homogenous (identical) products
        • Eg. fuels like petrol or diesel
    • Perception of quality
      • Products of a higher perceived quality are more competitive

Factors

Productivity

  • Output (GDP, revenue) per unit of input (labour cost, labour time, material costs, capital costs)
  • Higher productivity
    • Increases output per unit of input
    • Decreases average cost
    • Decreases prices of exports
    • Increases competitiveness

Inflation Rate

  • Percentage by which the price of goods and services in a country rise per year
  • Lower inflation rate relative to other countries
    • Decreases prices of exports relative to other countries
    • Increases competitiveness

Wage Rate

  • Amount paid to workers per unit of output or per unit of time spent working
  • Lower wage rate
    • Decreases cost of production
    • Decreases prices of exports
    • Increases competitiveness

Exchange Rate

  • Price of currency relative to another base currency (eg. $USD)
  • Lower exchange rate
    • Decreases price of producer's currency when converted to consumer's currency
    • Decreases prices of exports when converted to the consumer's currency
    • Increases competitiveness

Measurements

Real Unit Labour Costs

  • Wage rate divided by productivity
    • Higher wage rate increases real unit labour costs
    • Higher productivity decreases real unit labour costs
  • Lower real unit labour costs
    • Decreases cost of production
      • Increased productivity, more output from same input
      • Decreased wages or time spent working, same output from less input
    • Decreases price of exports
    • Increases competitiveness
  • Statistics
    • Decreased 6% since 2000
    • Increased Australia's competitiveness

Trade Weighted Exchange Rate

  • Also known as "Trade Weighted Index"
  • Exchange rate relative to trading partners
  • Lower trade weighted exchange rate
    • Decreased price of currency when converted to trading partners' currency
    • Decreased price of exports when converted to consumer's currency
    • Increases competitiveness
  • Statistics
    • Increased 57% since 2000
    • Increased Australia's competitiveness

Purchasing Power Parity

Definition

  • A good or service should cost the same amount in each country once converted to a common currency
  • Measure to determine if a currency is over or undervalued

Formula

  • Cost in country B (in country B's currency) divided by cost in country A (in country A's currency)
  • Ratio greater than 1
    • Currency overvalued
  • Ratio less than 1
    • Currency undervalued

Disadvantages

  • Not usually an accurate measurement of by how much a currency is over or undervalued
  • Country may have introduced some other condition which drives up the price of the product
    • Eg. for hamburgers, a tariff on imported beef products would artificially increase the price
