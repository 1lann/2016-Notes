
No Sugar
Jack Davis


Themes
- Asserts the horrifyingly racist undertones present in white soceity, and the
  blatant racial prejudice towards the Aboriginal people
- Challenges stereotypes of Aboriginal people held by white Australian society
- Celebrates the unprecedented resilience of the Aboriginal people in the face
  over 100 years of complete dispossession by white Australians

Racism

- Joe: "A steadfast performance of duty ... With them, a reminder of the
  dangers they faced, the in the shape of three lorries carrying Aborigines"
	- Joe reading from a newspaper about Australia Day celebrations
	- Represents white Australia's attitude towards Aboriginals, that they are
	  a "danger"
	- "lorries", as if hearded like cattle in large, amorphous groups
	- Congratulates those who took them from their families
	- The triumphant narrative is disempowered by Joe's hesitant dictation
		- Symbolic of how such supposed "achievements" in the eyes of white
		  society have had devasating consequences for the oppressed
		  Aborigines, lacking in education, poor living conditions, etc
	- Further disempowered by Jimmy's angered outbursts
		- Jimmy: "Cause them bastards took our country"


- Oombulgurri massacre
x Billy, stage direction: "He grunts and mimes pulling a trigger"
	- "Grunts", makes the action appear more brutal, as if committed by some
	  savage animals
		- Dehumanises his attackers
	- Multiple grunts: "He grunts and mimes this"
		- "He demonstrates violently"
	- By miming, he visibly demonstrates the violence to the audience, making
	  the events all the more shocking
- Billy: "They say they gettin' gugja"
	- "Gugja" are lilly roots for food
	- Massacre started over an insignificant act
	- Demonstrates the racial motivation behind the massacre
- Billy: "Not like my country, finish... finish"
	- How such an event has spiritually polluted the region
	- Billy has lost the vital connection he had to his land
		- Lost all heritage, culture, and sense of belonging
- Extended silences allowing the audience to reflect in horror upon the story
	- A new perspective of history, telling the story from an Aboriginal man's
	  perspective


x "Billy Kimberley ... dressed in a new but absurdly ill-fitting uniform"
	- Can be read as an allusion to Heart of Darkness
	- Fireman aboard the steamboat, "Like a dog in a parody of breeches and a
	  feather hat"
	- Emphasise how out of place Billy is, how far removed from his culture and
	  tradition, acquiescent in his subjugation, much like natives in Africa
	  under the control of European imperialists
	- How he has been forced to work in such a position out of necessity, and
	  how tragic his situation really is
		- No other ability to support himself
		- Lost his identity, culture, home, sense of belonging at the hands of
		  white civilisation and archaic colonialist attitudes


- Davis' reliance on historical evidence
- Legitimises the events in the play and the experiences of the characters
- Projects such vile racism as a true feature of history, rather than an
  exaggerated tale of fiction


- Constable: "Should put a pinch of strychnine (poison) in the flour"
	- Condoning genocide
	- Allusion to real life massacre
		- Strychnine used in the Bedford Downs masacre in 1924 to kill a group
		  of Aboriginal men for spearing a milk cow
	- Highlights how such acts are not merely jokes in a work of fiction, but
	  real life occurrences
- Sergeant: "Too late to adopt the Tasmanian solution"
	- "Tasmanian solution", allusion to a period of violent conflict between
	  British colonialists and Aboriginals, resulting in the near destruction
	  of the Tasmanian Aboriginal population
	- Ironic considering the Sergeant is the local protector of Aborigines, yet
	  jokes about genocide
	- To a modern audience, the use of the word "solution" recalls Hitler's
	  "final solution", the name given to the Nazi plan to exterminate all Jews
	  during World War 2
		- Evoke horror in audience at the severity of the Sergeant and the
		  Constable's racism
	- "solution", implying the Aboriginal people are a problem that must be
	  dealt with, completely denying them any humanity


- Mary: "Go to hell! Fuck youse!"
x Stage direction: "Billy holds her outstretched over a pile of flour bags.
  Neal raises the cat-'o-nine tails. Blackout. A scream."
	- Very confronting for the audience, such a vile act of hateful racism
	  resulting from Mary's defile of his power
		- First direct view of physical oppression for the audience
	- Billy
		- Acquiesce in his own subjugation
		- Lost all identity and culture
		- Forced out of necessity to work for whites
			- Tragic
	- Even in the stage directions, short sentences to shock a reader of the
	  play's script


- Displacement of Aboriginals from their land
- Jimmy: "Wetjalas ... don't want us 'ere ... and old Jimmy Mitchell's tight
  'coz they reckon Bert 'Awke's gonna give him a hidin' in the election"
	- Jimmy highlights racial motivations behind being forced to move from their
	  home
	- Demonstrates the perverse arrogance in white society, in forcing these
	  people to sever their deep connection to their land, disobeying their
	  culturally significant traditions
	- No risk of backlash from voters, as Aboriginal people are unable to vote
- Disregarded by policemen
	- Constable: "Bullshit", Sergeant: "Oh Jesus, shut up will youse?"
x Perambulatory stage
	- Force audience to move between stages during changes of setting in the
	  play
	- Symbolic of the forced distabilisation and displacement of Aboriginal
	  people, such as the Millimurra and Munday family in the play
	- Forcing the audience to consider the consequences of shifting these
	  people away from their home, severing the thousand year connection they
	  have with their land


- Pinjarra massacre
- Oombulgurri massacre juxtaposed against description of Pinjarra massacre by
  Auber Octavius Neville to the Western Australian Historical Society
- Neville: "The murder of isolated whites occurred during this period, with a
  heavy toll of black life being exacted in reprisal"
- "The official estimate was fifteen to twenty dead, but only eight women
  and several children were finally rounded up"
	- Tragic recount of a horrifying story, leaving the audience shocked by the
	  brutality of the act
	- "rounded up", connotations, as if the Aboriginal people are like cattle
	  to be hearded
- In a 1990 production of No Sugar by Neil Armfield and Lynette Narkle
	- Proceeding Neville's speech was a "vibrant corroboree"
	- "visible layer of unsettled red dust marked his presence as incongruous,
	   invasive and ultimately illegitimate" (scholar Helen Gilbert)
	- Tragic irony, in coldly recounting the death of these people after such
	  a vibrant display of such valuable culture
- "We are apt to refer somewhat scathingly to Tasmania's harshness in ridding
   herself of her natives"
	- "We", collective pronoun, as if he himself doesn't hold this opinion, but
	  is attempting to remain politically correct
x Stage direction: "He pauses and takes a drink of water"
	- Allows the audience a moment of appalled silence
	- Neville remains casual, seemingly unaware of his own hypocrisy in meaning
	  to "protect" these people, yet absolutely failing to do so


- Neville: "You are preparing yourselves here to take your place in Australian
  society"
	- Patronising
	- Play acknowledges the perverse irony in white society "civilising"
	  Aboriginals considering the lurid behaviour of many supposed teachers
x Neal, stage directions: "Mary brings him tea on a tray. He leers at her body"
	- Audience is disgusted at the vile behaviour of Neal
	- Ironic, as Neal is meant to be "civilising" Mary in her behaviour, yet
	  is a far more perverse and twisted being himself


- Jimmy yelling to Constable: "The roped off section for blackfellas. Al Jolson
  makin' out he was black, poor white bastard!"
	- Al Jolson in the movie The Jazz Singer from the 1920s
		- A white man playing a black character, coloured black using paint
	- Jimmy being sarcastic in expressing fake sympathy for Al Jolson for being
	  black
		- Recognises the inherent prejudice against Aboriginals
	- Highlights how Aboriginal people are aware of the racial prejudice that
	  exists in society and of their own marginalisation
	- Demonstrates Jimmy's propensity for outbursts of criticism against
	  authorities


x Sign (a prop): "Government of Western Australia, Fisheries, Forestry,
  Wildlife, and Aborigines"
	- Aboriginals listed as part of the flora and fauna
		- Subhuman
	- List appears in alphabetical order, yet "Aborigines" placed last
		- Even less significant than plants, trees and fish
	- Appears on a government sign, an organisation designed to represent the
	  people and their interests
		- Symbolic of the attitudes of white society towards Aboriginal people



Challenging Stereotypes

- One justification for removing Aboriginal children from their families (the
  Stolen Generation) was to "improve [the] child's conditions of living" (Peter
  Read, The Stolen Generations, 1981)
- Milly to David: "Gimme that shirt, it's filthy"
- Gran: "Eh! Now you take them clothes down the soak, you 'ear me?"
- Milly: "We takin' her to the doctor straight away" (Cissie's sick)
x Stage direction: "Cissie with the aid of a knife is looking for lice in
  David's hair"
	- Davis contradicts the attitude held by white society at this time that
	  Aboriginal families were unable to provide adequate living conditions for
	  their children
	- Whole family insisting on cleanliness, Gran demanding it aggressively
- Milly: "What am I gonna wash with? How can I keep my kids clean and sen 'em
  to school?"
	- Irony in removing soap from rations, then removing children from families
	  for squalid living conditions


- Belief that Aboriginals are lazy
- Sergeant: "Three healthy men bludging off you, too lazy to work"
	- Demonstrating white man belief that Aboriginal people are lazy


- Belief that Aboriginals are unable to concentrate
- Neal to Billy: "You wait, all right? Don't go walkabout"
	- "Walkabout" is a rite of passage for adolescent Aboriginal males, to
	  make the spiritual transition into manhood
	- Trivialising a significant part of their culture through misunderstanding
	  it
	- Ironic considering Neal's role



Celebration of Resilience

- Ability to adapt and survive no matter the circumstances
- How they attempt to sustain their culture, language, traditions despite the
  decades of violent dispossession


- Use of Aboriginal language, "dawarra", "mirri", "koorawoorung"
	- Highlights how they still attempt to retain vestiges of their culture in
	  modern Australia
	- Authenticates their experiences
	- Alienate audience from their culture
		- Empathise with Aboriginal people, in how they are excluded and
		  alienated from white Australian society
	- Sense of validation and inclusion felt by Nyoongah viewer of the play
		- An absent feeling from much of their interaction with white society


- David: "Woolah! Don Bradman" (p. 9, A1S1)
	- Oxymoron
	- Nyoongah shout of praise in reference to white sporting hero
	- Aboriginals have adapted to and adopted components of white Australian
	  culture, especially in their icons


x Stage direction, Joe: "He mimes throwing the doak at at a rabbit"
	- Hunting "rabits", an imported, non-native species, with traditional
	  Aboriginal hunting tools
	- Surviving by adapting traditional techniques to modern times, including
	  hunting new species


- Joe: "I'm gunna show you my country. Got a big river, swans, beautiful white
  swans"
	- Ironic, as "white swans" are an imported species, only black swans are
	  native
	- Appreciating the beauty of a symbol associated with the culture
	  oppressing him
